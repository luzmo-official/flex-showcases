The Great Global
Coffee Index

How 2.25 billion cups a day became the invisible engine of the world economy — and why your morning latte costs more than ever.

By Eleanor Vance & Marcus Hale | 12 min read | February 18, 2026

Every morning, before the world has even opened its eyes, something extraordinary happens. In São Paulo, a farmer checks his arabica cherries under a violet pre-dawn sky. In Helsinki, a software engineer queues for a third pour-over before nine. In Istanbul, a vendor prepares his brass cezve with the practiced rhythm of a thousand mornings. They are all part of the same vast, fragrant, slightly jittery machine: the global coffee economy.

Coffee is not merely a beverage. It is a $495 billion annual industry, a geopolitical force, a productivity crutch, and — depending on who you ask — either humanity's greatest invention or its most socially acceptable addiction. We set out to map this empire of the bean, cup by cup, country by country, dollar by dollar. What we found surprised us.

Analysis

The Nordic Paradox

The numbers tell a counterintuitive story. The world's heaviest coffee drinkers are not in Italy, Colombia, or even the United States — they are in Scandinavia. Finland, Norway, Iceland, and Denmark occupy the top four spots globally, consuming nearly three times the European average.

Researchers point to a constellation of factors: long, dark winters that demand stimulants; a cultural tradition of kahvitauko (the Finnish coffee break, essentially a constitutional right); and crucially, some of the world's lightest roasts, which paradoxically contain more caffeine than their darker Italian counterparts.

The implications are far-reaching. Finnish productivity data — among the highest in the OECD — correlates almost suspiciously with their caffeine intake. Correlation is not causation, of course. But try telling that to a Finn on their fifth cup.

Editor's Note Finland has twice attempted to ban coffee — in 1919 and again during WWII rationing. Both times, a thriving black market emerged almost overnight. The Finnish relationship with coffee is, to put it mildly, non-negotiable.

Deep Dive

The Rise of the $7 Latte

In 2015, the average specialty coffee in a major Western city cost $3.80. Today, that number has nearly doubled. In Zurich, Copenhagen, and San Francisco, a single-origin oat milk latte with house-made vanilla now routinely breaks the $7 barrier. In parts of Tokyo's Shibuya district, it climbs past $9.

The drivers are layered: climate-driven supply shocks in Brazil and Vietnam (which together produce 55% of the world's coffee), the premiumisation of café culture, rising real estate costs for urban coffee shops, and a generational shift toward "experience spending." Millennials and Gen Z consumers report spending 40% more on coffee than their parents did at the same age — adjusted for inflation.

But here's what's genuinely remarkable: demand hasn't flinched. Global consumption has risen every single year for the past decade, regardless of price increases. Economists call this "inelastic demand." The rest of us call it addiction.

Conclusion

The Bottom of the Cup

The data paints a portrait of a species that is, by any honest measure, completely dependent on a small tropical seed. We have built a half-trillion-dollar global infrastructure around it. We have fought wars over it, written symphonies about it, and structured our entire working day around its rhythms.

The coffee industry faces genuine headwinds — climate change threatens to reduce suitable growing regions by up to 50% by 2050, labour shortages persist across Central American plantations, and the gap between what consumers pay and what farmers earn remains morally troubling. These are stories this data cannot fully tell, but that the numbers quietly insist we confront.

And yet, tomorrow morning, 2.25 billion cups will be poured again. The farmer will check his cherries. The engineer will queue for her pour-over. The vendor will heat his cezve. The machine hums on — one cup at a time, perpetually, magnificently, stubbornly caffeinated.
